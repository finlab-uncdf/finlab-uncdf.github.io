Affinity Mapping

30 Min

About

Affinity Mapping is a synthesis tool aimed at identifying patterns in the research observations and findings by clustering group observations and research data into logical, thematic categories of learning and insight.

Use Cases

  • Structuring the team's thinking on a given topic being researched.
  • Organising the research findings into distinct opportunity areas.
  • Discussing the research and how the experiences varied from one respondent to the next.

Limitations

This is a great way to take a first stab at organising insights from the research however it can be difficult to be exhaustive. If the opportunity clusters that emerge don‘t feel tangible enough, consider trying a couple other methods to look at the data from a new angle.

Affinity Mapping | PlaybookUX

Understand

  • The ‘Observation‘, ‘Quote‘, ‘Takeway’ should all be parts of the secondary and primary research the team has conducted. Any valuable information should be written down on a sticky note and added to the wall.
  • The ‘Themes’ will emerge from grouping similar takeaways or observations together. There is no set rule to identify a theme, rather it is a matter of making sure each theme cluster concisely captures an aspect of the given experience.

Step by Step

  1. Review the research: Ask your team members to silently note down observations, quotes and other relevant data points from your respective research activities onto separate sticky notes.
  2. Place, discuss, group: Each person should then describe each of their notes to the others and start adding them to the wall. As related points come up start forming clusters.
  3. Unpack clusters: If at any point there seem to be multiple themes in one cluster the cluster should be broken down into smaller themes.
  4. Review wall: Before wrapping up, review the clusters with the group, make sure everyone understands what they mean and check if anything is missing.

Facilitators Section

How to for Facilitators

  1. At the start: Have everyone spend time silently going over their research notes and write down valuable information on sticky notes.
  2. During the exercise: Help the participants form clusters, feel free to make suggestions for new clusters or ask if some can be broken down further.
  3. When summarising: Have participants present to other groups how they‘ve structured their own map.

Facilitators Question Bank

  • You brought up [observation], where might that fit onto the map?
  • What clusters do we have so far? Can you walk me through them?
  • Should any cluster be broken down further? How about this one?
  • Has anything come up that felt new, surprising, refreshing? If so call it out on your map by circling it.
  • Are there things that can sit under two or more themes? That's fine, make sure they are represented in both clusters.
  • What are the most important themes to surface so far?
